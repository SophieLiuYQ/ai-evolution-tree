## How Llama 1 65B compares to peers

| Model | Date | Org | Params | MMLU | Released | License |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| GPT-3 | 2020-05 | OpenAI | 175B | 43.9% | API only | proprietary |
| Gopher | 2021-12 | DeepMind | 280B | 60.0% | no | proprietary |
| Chinchilla | 2022-03 | DeepMind | 70B | 67.5% | no | proprietary |
| PaLM | 2022-04 | Google | 540B | 69.3% | API only | proprietary |
| **Llama 1 65B** | **2023-02** | **Meta** | **65B** | **63.4%** | **weights (research)** | **research-only → leaked** |
| Llama 2 70B | 2023-07 | Meta | 70B | 68.9% | weights | commercial OK |
| Llama 3 70B | 2024-04 | Meta | 70B | 82.0% | weights | commercial OK |

Llama 1 hit a quality similar to Chinchilla at a smaller (and open) parameter count. The leak made it the first frontier-comparable model anyone could download and run locally.

## The leak and its consequences

Meta initially distributed Llama via a request form gated to vetted researchers. Within seven days, a torrent containing all four model sizes appeared on 4chan; within a month, it was on Hugging Face. Meta's response was telling: rather than legal action, they spent the next four months preparing Llama 2 — released with a permissive commercial license. The pivot from "leak crisis" to "open is the strategy" defined Meta's AI positioning for the next 3 years.
