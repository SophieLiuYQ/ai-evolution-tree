## The under-training table

| Model | Params | Tokens | Tokens / Param | Chinchilla-optimal? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| GPT-3 | 175B | 300B | 1.7× | drastically under-trained |
| Gopher | 280B | 300B | 1.1× | drastically under-trained |
| Megatron-Turing NLG | 530B | 270B | 0.5× | extremely under-trained |
| **Chinchilla** | **70B** | **1.4T** | **20×** | **optimal** |
| Llama 1 65B | 65B | 1.4T | 22× | ~optimal |
| Llama 2 70B | 70B | 2.0T | 29× | over-trained (deliberately, for inference efficiency) |
| Llama 3 70B | 70B | 15T | 215× | vastly over-trained (inference efficiency dominates) |

Modern models deliberately over-train beyond Chinchilla-optimal because training cost is a one-time expense while inference cost compounds forever.

## Why the paper lands in the tree, not as a footnote

Chinchilla didn't just correct a scaling exponent. It reshaped how the industry allocates AI capital. The observation "data is the bottleneck, not parameters" directly triggered the 2023-24 push toward proprietary data licensing, synthetic data generation, and the legal fights (NYT v OpenAI, Getty v Stability) that are still unresolved as of 2026.
