## Why this enables depth

Before ResNet, training networks past ~20 layers degraded performance — counterintuitively, deeper networks performed *worse*. The cause was vanishing gradients during backprop: gradient signal multiplicatively shrank as it passed through many layers.

Skip connections give gradients a multiplication-free shortcut path back to early layers. The chain rule for a residual block becomes:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_{i+1}} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{\partial \mathcal{F}}{\partial x_i}\right)
$$

The "+1" guarantees gradients flow even when $\mathcal{F}$'s gradient is small. Networks of 152, 1001, or even more layers became trainable.

## ResNet's downstream descendants

| Year | Architecture | Inheritance |
|---|---|---|
| 2016 | DenseNet | each layer connects to ALL previous |
| 2017 | Highway Networks → Transformer | residuals + LayerNorm in transformer blocks |
| 2017 | MobileNet | depthwise-separable convs + skip connections |
| 2019 | EfficientNet | compound scaling depth/width/resolution |
| 2020 | ViT (Vision Transformer) | residual transformer blocks for vision |
| 2024 | every deep network | residuals as default architectural primitive |

ResNet's specific architecture is mostly historical for production CV (replaced by ConvNeXt, ViT, and Swin), but the residual-connection idea is universal.
