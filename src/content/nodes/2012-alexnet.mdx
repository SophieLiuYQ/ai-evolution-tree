## What it is

AlexNet is an 8-layer convolutional neural network (5 conv + 3 fully-connected) that won the 2012 ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge with a top-5 error rate of 15.3% — the runner-up scored 26.2%. The 11-percentage-point margin was unprecedented and forced the field to take deep learning seriously.

The technical paper introduced **no single new idea** — convolutions (Fukushima 1980), backprop (1986), ReLU (Nair & Hinton 2010), dropout (Hinton et al. 2012), GPU training (multiple precursors) all predated it. AlexNet's contribution was **engineering**: it combined them at a scale that, given the available data (ImageNet) and hardware (two GTX 580s), produced a result no prior system could match.

## How it works

Architectural ingredients:

| Layer | Output | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Conv1 | 96 × 55 × 55 | 11 × 11 stride 4, ReLU |
| Pool1 | 96 × 27 × 27 | max pool 3 × 3 stride 2 |
| Conv2 | 256 × 27 × 27 | 5 × 5, grouped across two GPUs |
| Pool2 | 256 × 13 × 13 | |
| Conv3-5 | 384 / 384 / 256 × 13 × 13 | 3 × 3 |
| FC6-7 | 4096 each | with dropout(0.5) |
| FC8 | 1000 | softmax |

Crucial techniques:
- **ReLU activations** instead of sigmoid/tanh — gradients flow, training converges much faster.
- **Dropout** in fully-connected layers — randomly zero 50% of activations during training; combats overfitting.
- **Data augmentation** — random crops + horizontal flips multiply effective dataset size.
- **Local response normalization** (since deprecated; BatchNorm replaced it).
- **Two-GPU model parallelism** — the model didn't fit in one GTX 580's 3 GB of VRAM, so layers were split across two GPUs.

## How to use it (today)

You don't train AlexNet from scratch in 2026 — you'd use a ResNet, ConvNeXt, or Vision Transformer. But it's still the first thing taught in any CV course:

```python
import torch
from torchvision.models import alexnet, AlexNet_Weights

model = alexnet(weights=AlexNet_Weights.IMAGENET1K_V1)
model.eval()

# inference on a 224x224 RGB image tensor
with torch.no_grad():
    logits = model(image_batch)
```

## SWOT

- **Strengths** — Demonstrated that depth + GPU compute + sufficient data is the recipe. Established CNNs as the default vision architecture for the next ~10 years. Made dropout and ReLU mainstream.
- **Weaknesses** — Tiny by 2026 standards (60M parameters). The architecture is no longer competitive — ResNet (2015), EfficientNet (2019), ViT (2020) all surpass it.
- **Opportunities** — As a teaching tool: every student should still implement and train it. Forces understanding of conv arithmetic, GPU memory, and overfitting.
- **Threats** — Vision transformers have largely displaced CNNs in research and increasingly in production.

## Limitations

- **Required hand-tuned hyperparameters** — learning rate schedule, weight decay, momentum, augmentation choices. The "no free lunch" of pre-2015 deep learning.
- **No transfer learning yet codified** — the "pretrain on ImageNet, fine-tune on X" recipe became conventional only after AlexNet showed the features were generally useful.
- **Architectural quirks** like grouped convolutions across GPUs were hardware-imposed, not principled.

## What it changed

Within 24 months of AlexNet:
- ImageNet leaderboards stopped accepting non-deep-learning entries (everyone switched).
- Google acquired DNNresearch (Hinton's lab) for ~$44M — perhaps the highest-ROI talent acquisition of the decade.
- NVIDIA's roadmap pivoted hard toward ML — leading directly to CUDA library development (cuDNN, 2014), Tensor Cores (2017), and the data center GPU as the dominant product line.

This single paper is a fair candidate for "highest dollars-per-page in scientific literature."
